Detailed comments for the authors This paper combines two popular approaches in machine learning i.e. kernel embedding of distributions, and spectral methods for latent parameter estimation.
In this paper, authors present a kernel method to learn multiview latent model. The observed variables in this multiview model are assumed to be conditionally independent given a discrete latent variable. The idea is to embed the join distribution of the variables into a kernel space, giving a high order covariance operator, on which spectral methods are applied to recover the latent parameters.

The paper is well written, though the symmetric view consideration causes confusion at some places, especially in section 5.2. It is not apparent how multiple views x_1, x_2, x_3 ultimately give the same conditional distribution? It would be useful to point it out right after the section 5.2.

In their motivation, authors contrast their work with respect to Sgouritsa et al. (2013) and Song et al. (2011). It would be more useful to compare this work directly with these, esp with Sgouritsa et al. (2013). This becomes more important since all other baselines seems obviously inferior especially baseline 3 and 4.
Summary of evaluation / Paper's main strengths and weaknesses Overall a nice paper. Experiments can be improved.

Detailed comments for the authors One can see a clear improvement compared to the previous version, though, there are also a number of new and annoying mistakes which demonstrate a sloppy work style of the authors. For instance, using the 1-dim kernel density rates for d-dim kernel density estimation; seriously?

There are a number of errors in the density estimation part in remark 2 -- some of which I assume to be non-trivial to fix -- and I'm thinking it might be better to cut the whole unsupervised density estimation part. I understand that it is a nice punch line but you had two shots at it and both were not very convincing. Given this history I'm not comfortable with a third, unreviewed, try and I suggest that you move this part into the summary section and state that guarantees are an object of future studies.

Detailed comments:

- There is an error in (7). This only holds for d=1. Use the dimension dependent rates in Wasserman or Silverman. This error carries through to Remark 3 (p.6) => Adjust the rates. I also don't appreciate the sentence "if the smoothness level alpha of the true density grows as O(d)"; what is this supposed to mean? And obviously, you don't get the O(m^{-1/3}) rate. Fair enough to have the dimension dependent rate but don't try to oversell.

- Another error in remark 3: You have no fixed RKHS since you use a bandwidth parameter and there are no guarantees anymore that the mean embedding converges or better that it converges with rate m^{-1/2}. You are using your Thm. 2 but this will certainly expect a fixed kernel -- otherwise I would be very doubtful of Thm. 2. Making Thm. 2 to hold simultaneously over a family of kernels (that is what you got if you use a bandwidth factor) should not be trivial. Even for the standard convergence of the mean embedding it is not obvious how to do that.
Summary of evaluation / Paper's main strengths and weaknesses The paper improved, though, the authors introduced a number of new errors. I suggest that the authors leave the guarantees for conditional density estimation as future work and change the text accordingly.

Detailed comments for the authors This paper brings together two emerging themes: the first pertains to the resurgence of the method of moments for estimating latent variable models using approximate tensor decomposition schemes (Anandkumar, Hsu, Kakade et al, 2012), and the second theme pertains to RKHS embeddings of probability distributions for extending spectral techniques to non-parametric estimation settings (Smola et al, 2007 and several recent papers). The focus in this paper is on "multi-view" latent variable models where observed variables are conditionally independent given a discrete hidden variable. Moment tensors in the kernelized settings are replaced by higher order covariance operators, whose tensor decomposition after appropriate whitening steps yields the mean embeddings of the underlying conditional distributions. The algorithm can be implemented on Gram matrices given a set of observations. The paper extends previous sample complexity results for this kernelized setting and some promising empirical results are reported.

Overall, the paper is well written and the contributions follow naturally from previous related literature.

Do other models such as ICA or LDA also admit such a kernelization?

Section 5.2 should be rewritten with pure kernelized notation (no feature spaces - only Gram matrices). Is Algorithm 1 only a partial description - e.g., I do not see the tensor power method invoked anywhere there. As I understand it, the power method is run on deflated versions of the tensor for i=1-to-k. Can these steps be entirely implemented with Gram matrices? If so please clarify, as such a full description will help appreciate the full computation complexity of the proposed algorithm. For the case of p views, are these Gram matrices of size pm x pm - if so scalability becomes a concern rather quickly?

Last line of the paper: "more robustness" -> "more robust"

It may be useful to provide a short proof sketch for Theorem 2, or atleast how the theorem relates to previous results. What is being added to the proof technique in this specific kernelized context? This will help make the paper a bit more self-contained.

In Figure 4, the benefits of the kernel spectral approach over EM-GMM are not consistent. How was the claim that these are datasets where the multiview assumption is "heavily violated" verified? As in Figure 2, is Hsu et al not applicable on this problem for some reason? It would have made the paper stronger to provide more empirical support on real datasets.
Summary of evaluation / Paper's main strengths and weaknesses Nice paper combining RKHS embedding ideas with tensor decomposition techniques for latent variable estimation - though in some respects the algorithmic extensions are straightforward. The proposed approach is backed with empirical results and theoretical analyses. The empirical section could be strengthened. The paper can be improved by making some sections more self-contained and clarifying precisely how some of the analysis builds on prior results.
